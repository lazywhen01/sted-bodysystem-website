Lifestyle that affect the circulatory and respiratory system


Lifestyle is defined as how individuals live their lives. It includes their daily behaviors, choices, and habits. Lifestyle encompasses a range of factors including diet, physical activity, sleep patterns, stress management, social interactions, and substance use. These factors affect the overall well-being and health status of an individual. Below are lifestyles that affect the respiratory and circulatory systems.

Smoking

Smoking involves the intentional act of inhaling and exhaling the smoke produced from tobacco rolled into cigarettes, cigars, or pipers. Cigarettes have a content of toxic chemicals; hence they are harmful, especially to the respiratory health. The harmful substances in cigarettes include nicotine, which is the primary addictive agent that makes smokers dependent on it, tar, which is a sticky substance that coats and damages lung tissues over time, carbon monoxide, which reduces oxygen delivery to the cells, and other thousands of compounds, including carcinogens or the cancer-causing substances.

People who do not smoke can be affected by smoking as well. Passive smoking or secondhand smoking exposure happens when a person breathes in cigarette smoke blown by a smoker. Even brief exposure to secondhand smoke is harmful and can cause health problems to the individual.


Tobacco usage in the Philippines

A Global Adult Tobacco Survey (GATS) conducted by the Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA) and the Department of Health (DOH) IN 2021, revealed that 1 in 5, or 15.1 million Filipino individuals aged 15 years and older, are current tobacco users. Male smokers have eight times higher percentage than female smokers. It was also concluded in the GATS that there has been a decline in tobacco users from 29.7% in 2009 to 19.5% in 2021. On average, daily smokers consume about 10 cigarettes.

It was also found that 2.5 million individuals were exposed to tobacco smoke in their respective workplaces. Moreover, about 16.8 million were exposed to tobacco smoke inside their homes. Filipinos are also exposed to secondhand smoke in public places.

Executive Order No. 26, entitled Providing Establishment of Smoke-Free Environments in Public and Eclosed Places (nationwide smoking ban order) was implemented by President Rodrigo Duterte on May 16, 2017. These mandates provide a smoke-free environment, conducting information dissemination and awareness campaigns to educate the public about the harmful effects of smoking, and penalizing those who do not comply. Minors are also protected from purchasing, selling, and distributing tobacco products.

Health Consequences from Tobacco Use

The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that more than 8 million individuals die from tobacco use every year, as of November 2023. From this, 1.3 million are non-smokers who were exposed to secondhand smoke.

Data from the Lung Center of the Philippines (LCP), shows that an average of 321 deaths caused by tobacco-related diseases were recorded, which amounts to 112,000 deaths yearly.

Respiratory Diseases Caused by Smoking:


Lung Cancer - all smokers (firsthand & secondhand) are at risk for lung cancer. The leading cause of this disease is tobacco use.

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - it is a chronic inflammatory lung disease, which restricts natural airflow in the body causing difficulty breathing in individuals. Smoking accounts for 70% of COPD cases. This disease is also called emphysema or chronic bronchitis.

Circulatory Diseases Caused by Smoking:


Heart Disease - smoking not only affects the lungs, but it also targets other organs in the body including the heart. Smoking causes blockage and narrows the arteries, making it harder for the blood and oxygen to flow to your heart.

Stroke - a disease caused by either a blocked artery in the brain (ischemic stroke) or the leaking or bursting of a blood vessel in the brain (hemorrhagic stroke). Smoking increases the risk of developing a stroke by contributing to the formation of blood clots and damaged blood vessels in the body.

Alcohol Intake

Alcoholic beverages are any drink that contains ethanol. It is classified as a drug, a central nervous system depressant that greatly impacts public health. Moderate consumption of alcoholic beverages may have mild effects, like relaxation and social inhibition.

However, excessive alcohol consumption can lead to a range of health problems including liver disease, heart disease, addiction, and mental health disorders. This lifestyle can affect the long-term health of the individual. While it is socially acceptable and legal to consume alcohol, it is important to recognize its potential risks and consequences when it is consumed irresponsibly.

Alcohol use in the Philippines

A survey conducted by the Department of Health in 2021 showed that an estimated 40% of Filipino adults drink alcohol, 33% of which are heavy drinkers. Drinking or inuman has been part of almost every Family, friendship, and community. It was revealed in a study that drinking has been part of their lifestyle: 45.5% of respondents said they drink alcohol to socialize, 23.8% drink to remove stress and burden, and 17.5% say they drink to be happy and gain pleasure.

The legal drinking age in the Philippines is 18, but minors can easily purchase alcoholic drinks in stores. Children start drinking as early as 15 years old. Data from the World Health Organization indicates that almost 9% of the Filipino population who are 15 years and older have an alcohol use disorder.

Death attributed to drinking among Filipino individuals is estimated to be 39,802 in the year 2019. Moreover, 25% of fatalities in traffic accidents are thought to be caused by drunk driving. And because excessive alcohol use can lower inhibitions and cloud a person’s judgments, there is a higher risk of committing violent offenses and displaying aggressive behavior.

Alcohol use in the Philippines

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), alcohol is classified as a psychoactive substance that affects the way a person thinks and behaves. Alcohol consumption is also a causal factor in more than 200 diseases, injuries, and adverse health conditions among individuals.

Respiratory Diseases Caused by Alcoholism:


Asthma - alcoholic beverages contain substances called histamines and sulfites, which may cause asthma symptoms in some people.

Choking or aspiration pneumonia - excessive alcohol intake increases the risk of vomiting. A person under alcohol intoxication is at higher risk of vomiting and inhaling their vomit, which can lead to choking or aspiration pneumonia.

Lung Injury and Disease - heavy alcohol consumption can reduce levels of the antioxidant glutathione, which protects the smaller airways of the lungs from oxidative damage. Excessive alcohol intake makes the lungs more prone to serious injury and disease.

Circulatory Diseases Caused by Alcoholism:


Stroke - excessive alcohol consumption can increase a person’s risk of stroke. Alcohol abuse is linked to over 1 million strokes each year, globally.

High blood pressure - is the most common alcohol-related health problem. Drinking a large amount of alcohol can affect the muscles in the blood vessels, which makes them narrower, making the risk of developing hypertension higher.

Heat Failure - prolonged alcohol abuse weakens the heart muscle and impairs its ability to pump blood effectively, leading to heart failure.

Sedentary Lifestyle

A sedentary lifestyle or inactive lifestyle refers to a way of living that involves minimal to no physical activity. A person who has a sedentary lifestyle spends six or more hours per day either sitting or lying down, lacking significant physical movement in their day-to-day life. An example of sedentary behavior is sitting at a desk at school or work, playing computer games or surfing the internet at home, and driving a car or sitting in a vehicle when traveling.

According to data from the World Health Organization (WHO), sedentary lifestyles have increased prevalently worldwide. The advancement of technology had a significant impact on promoting sedentary behavior. Devices such as computers, smartphones, and televisions offer a sedentary form of entertainment and communication making people spend more time engaged in screen-based activities.

Health Consequences of Sedentary Lifestyle

The World Health Organization (WHO) emphasized that physical inactivity can cause severe effects on people’s health. According to WHO, 60 to 85% of people in the world lead sedentary lifestyles, making it one of the more serious yet insufficiently addressed public health issues. A sedentary lifestyle doubles the risk of acquiring cardiovascular diseases, diabetes, obesity, and even depression and anxiety.

Effects on the Respiratory System:


Decreased Lung Capacity - prolonged periods of physical inactivity can lead to the weakening of the respiratory muscles, resulting in a reduced ability to take deep breaths and a decreased lung capacity.

Reduced Oxygen Efficiency - individuals with a sedentary lifestyle may have lower oxygen utilization efficiency. Their bodies are less efficient at extracting and using oxygen from the air they breathe.

Increased Risk of Respiratory Conditions - a sedimentary lifestyle is associated with an increased risk of developing respiratory conditions such as asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and respiratory infections.

Effects on the Circulatory System:


Decreased Cardiovascular Fitness - lack of physical activity can lead to a decline in cardiovascular fitness, which is the ability of the heart, blood vessels, and lungs to supply oxygen-rich blood to the body’s tissues during physical activity.

Increased blood pressure - sedentary individuals are more likely to develop high blood pressure (hypertension), which can strain the heart and blood vessels, increasing the risk of heart disease and stroke.

Altered Blood Lipid Levels - a sedentary lifestyle can contribute to an unfavorable blood lipid profile, with higher levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol and lower levels of high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol, increasing the risk of atherosclerosis and cardiovascular diseases.

Unhealthy Diet

Diet is one of the essential factors that affect the health of an individual. The food that we consume is the source of nutrients that we need to grow and perform bodily functions. A diet is considered unhealthy if it is high in unhealthy components like calories, refined sugar, saturated and trans-fat, and sodium. These foods undergo a lot of processes stripping off their nutritional value.

Highly processed goods are the most common type of unhealthy foods. This can include items like fast foods, canned goods, junk foods, and highly-sweetened beverages. Eating a healthier option of food is not always easy, as raw food prices for meat, fish, and even fruits and vegetables are more expensive than processed ones. Lower-income households often rely more on cheap, convenient, processed foods, hence there is a high amount of malnourished or under nutrition children. In some cases, consuming highly processed foods daily leads to obesity and chronic diseases.

Effects on the Respiratory System:


High Blood Pressure - foods high in sodium, salt, and trans-fat can contribute to the development of hypertension, which is a risk factor for heart disease and stroke.

Atherosclerosis - excessive amounts of saturated and trans-fat can raise levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) leading to the buildup of plaque in the arteries, which can cause heart attacks and strokes.

Obesity and Insulin Resistance - an unhealthy diet can lead to obesity and insulin resistance which are factors for developing cardiovascular diseases.

Effects on the Circulatory System:


Decreased Cardiovascular Fitness - lack of physical activity can lead to a decline in cardiovascular fitness, which is the ability of the heart, blood vessels, and lungs to supply oxygen-rich blood to the body’s tissues during physical activity.

Increased blood pressure - sedentary individuals are more likely to develop high blood pressure (hypertension), which can strain the heart and blood vessels, increasing the risk of heart disease and stroke.

Altered Blood Lipid Levels - a sedentary lifestyle can contribute to an unfavorable blood lipid profile, with higher levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol and lower levels of high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol, increasing the risk of atherosclerosis and cardiovascular diseases.

Poor Sleeping Habits

Poor Sleeping Habits refer to the behavior and routines that disrupt the quality and quantity of sleep a person gets. This lifestyle can hurt the overall health of an individual. it is important to follow the recommended sleep duration for different age groups to have a night of good-quality sleep.

One of the primary causes of poor sleeping habits is the presence of technology in our daily lives. From television to smartphones, to laptops, and gaming consoles, these technological devices have become a part of our routine. When used excessively, without limitation, these can lead to disrupted sleep patterns and compromised sleep quality. Technology become so addictive, that some people sacrifice their sleep over screen-time activities.

Sleep deprivation happens when a person fails to get the right amount of sleep that they need. When this gets severe or if it happens over an extended period, it can eventually lead to disruptive symptoms that interfere with the daily life of a person. It could also heighten the risk of major health complications. In a Philips survey in 2021, among Asian countries, Filipinos have one of the highest rates of sleep deprivation; 46% of Filipinos do not get enough sleep.

Health Consequences of Poor Sleeping Habits

Effects on the Respiratory System:


Impaired Lung Function - insufficient sleep can reduce lung capacity, lower oxygen saturation levels, and impair the lungs’ ability to clear mucus and other airway irritants which can lead to an increased risk of respiratory distress.

Increased Risk of Pulmonary Hypertension - research has connected poor sleep quality and duration to the onset and development of pulmonary hypertension. Poor sleeping habits can disrupt the normal regulation of blood vessels in the lungs, which leads to constriction and elevated pressure.

Increased Risk of Respiratory Infections - sleep deprivation weakens the immune system, making the individuals more prone to respiratory infections like the common cold, flu, and pneumonia.

Effects on the Circulatory System:


Coronary Heart Disease - deprivation can contribute to factors such as inflammation and hypertension which are associated with an elevated risk of coronary heart disease.

High Blood Pressure - when the body lacks sleep, it releases more stress hormones that can narrow the blood vessels and make the heart work harder to pump blood. This can lead to hypertension, or high blood pressure, a major risk factor for heart disease and stroke.

Stroke - getting enough sleep over time leads to disruption in the blood vessels and increased inflammation in the body. When this happens, blood clots may form which can block blood flow to the brain, leading to stroke.
