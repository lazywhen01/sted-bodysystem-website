What is Circulatory System?

The Circulatory System transports blood from the heart to the lungs for oxygenation, then distributes oxygen-rich blood throughout the body via arteries. Veins return oxygen-depleted blood to the heart, maintaining the circulation cycle essential for the health of organs, muscles, and tissues.

The circulatory system comprises your heart and blood vessels. Its primary role is to deliver oxygen, nutrients, and hormones to muscles, tissues, and organs across your body.

Additionally, it aids in eliminating waste from cells and organs, facilitating their disposal by the body. The circulatory system can also be referred to as your cardiovascular system. Cardio means heart, and vascular refers to blood vessels.

1. Heart The heart pumps blood around the body. It sits inside the chest, in front of the lungs, and slightly to the left side. The heart is a double pump made up of four chambers, with the flow of blood going in one direction due to the presence of the heart valves. The contractions of the chambers make the sound of heartbeats.

The right side of the heart

The right upper chamber (atrium) receives deoxygenated blood rich in carbon dioxide, which is then pumped into the right ventricle and transported via an artery to the lungs, where oxygen replaces the carbon dioxide.

The left side of the heart

After being oxygenated, the blood returns to the heart, entering the left atrium, and then passing into the left ventricle before being sent through the aorta, a major artery. This marks the beginning of its circulation throughout the body once again.


2. Blood Vessels

What are the types of blood vessels?

Arteries, slender muscular vessels, transport oxygen-rich blood from the heart to various body regions. The aorta, the largest artery, originates from the heart, extending upwards through the chest (ascending aorta) and downwards into the abdomen (descending aorta). From the aorta, the coronary arteries diverge, subsequently dividing into smaller arterioles as they distance themselves from the heart.

Veins, are responsible for transporting deoxygenated blood back to the heart. They begin as small vessels known as venules and increase in size as they near the heart. The heart receives blood from two major veins: the superior vena cava, which conveys blood from the upper body (including the head and arms), and the inferior vena cava, which carries blood from the lower body (including the stomach, pelvis, and legs). Valves in leg veins prevent the backward flow of blood.

Capillaries, are tiny blood vessels that bridge the gap between small arteries (arterioles) and veins (venules). They feature delicate walls permitting the exchange of oxygen, carbon dioxide, nutrients, and waste products between cells.


3. Blood Transports the following substances: Gases, namely oxygen (O2) and carbon dioxide (CO2), between the lungs and the rest of the body.

Blood consists of:

1. Red Blood Cells Erythrocytes, commonly referred to as red blood cells, transport oxygen to body tissues where it is converted into energy, while simultaneously carrying carbon dioxide from tissues to the lungs for exhalation.

2. White Blood Cells White blood cells, or leukocytes, play a vital role in defending the body against infections. Operating within the immune system, they move through the bloodstream and react to injuries or diseases.

3. Platelets, also known as thrombocytes, are tiny, colorless fragments found in the bloodstream, responsible for clot formation to halt or prevent bleeding. Produced within the bone marrow, a spongy tissue housed inside bones, platelets originate from stem cells which give rise to red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets

4. Plasma, Plasma, the fluid component of blood, acts as a carrier, delivering nutrients to cells across different organs and transporting waste generated by cellular activities to organs like the kidneys, liver, and lungs for removal.

What are the circulatory system circuits?

The circulatory system consists of three circuits through which blood continuously flows in a cycle, passing through the heart.

1. The pulmonary circuit transports deoxygenated blood from the heart to the lungs, while oxygenated blood is returned to the heart via the pulmonary veins.

2. Systemic circuit, oxygenated blood containing nutrients and hormones is pumped from the heart to all parts of the body. As the body consumes oxygen, nutrients, and hormones, waste products are collected in the veins.

3. Coronary circuit, which pertains to the arteries of the heart, supplies oxygenated blood to the cardiac muscle. Subsequently, it transports oxygen-depleted blood back to the right atrium of the heart, from where it is sent to the lungs for oxygenation.
