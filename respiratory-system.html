What is Respiratory System?

The respiratory system is the biological system in organisms that enables breathing and exchange of gases between the body and the environment. It includes several organs and structures involved in the process of respiration, which is essential for supplying oxygen to the body's cells and removing carbon dioxide, a waste product of cellular metabolism.


Quick Fact!

The rate at which human beings breathe is 12-18 breaths per minute. There are 1440 minutes in 24 hours, therefore, the average number of breaths drawn in by a human (at a rate of 12 breaths per minute) in a day is 17,280.

The main function of the respiratory system is to allow gas exchange (oxygen and carbon dioxide) with different parts of the body. The different organs of the respiratory system are the nose, pharynx, epiglottis, larynx, trachea, bronchi, and lungs. These organs carry out the process of respiration.

The respiratory system comprises the body’s organs and components responsible for the process of breathing, facilitating the exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide. Every cell in your body requires oxygen for functioning. As they absorb oxygen, they produce carbon dioxide, known as a “waste gas,” which enters the bloodstream and is transported to the lungs. Upon exhalation, you release this carbon dioxide by breathing it out. This vital function is called “gas exchange,” and your body is set up to do it automatically.

Sinuses - The sinuses are empty cavities within the skull bones, linked to the nasal passage by tiny openings. They assist in controlling the temperature and moisture levels of the air you inhale, while also reducing the weight of the skull and contributing to vocal resonance.

Nasal Cavity - The nasal cavity, or nose, serves as the primary gateway for external air to enter your respiratory system. The hairs lining its interior wall play a role in the system’s air purification process.

Oral Cavity - Air can also enter through your mouth, known as the oral cavity, particularly in cases of habitual mouth breathing or temporary blockage of the nasal passages.

Adenoids - The adenoids are enlarged lymphoid tissue located at the upper part of the throat. In cases where they obstruct breathing, they may be surgically removed. The lymphatic system, comprising nodes and vessels, transports fluid throughout the body, aiding in infection resistance by filtering foreign substances, such as bacteria, and generating lymphocytes to combat them.

Tonsils - The tonsils are lymph nodes located in the pharynx wall. They are not a crucial component of the body’s immune system. In cases of infection, they may be surgically removed.

Pharynx - The pharynx, also known as the throat, gathers air entering from your nose and directs it downwards towards your trachea, or windpipe.

Epiglottis - The epiglottis is a tissue flap that protects the opening of your trachea. It seals shut when swallowing to ensure that substances are directed to the esophagus and stomach, rather than the airway.

Larynx - The larynx, or voice box, houses your vocal cords. As air moves in and out during breathing, it produces vocal sounds.

Esophagus - The esophagus is the pathway that connects the mouth and throat to the stomach.

Trachea - The trachea, also known as the windpipe, is the passage that extends from the pharynx to the lungs.

Ribs - The ribs are skeletal structures that provide support and protection to the chest cavity. They undergo slight movement and aid in the expansion and contraction of the lungs.

Bronchi - The trachea splits into two primary tubes known as bronchi, with each one leading to a lung. These bronchi then branch out into smaller tubes called bronchioles.

Pleura - The pleura consists of two membranes that enclose each lobe of the lungs, providing a separation between the lungs and the chest wall.

Cilia - Cilia (like very small hairs) that have a wave-like motion.

Mucus - Mucus, a viscous liquid or sticky phlegm, is propelled upward and expelled into the throat where it is either coughed out or swallowed. It serves to trap and retain dust, germs, and other undesirable particles that have entered the lungs. The expulsion of mucus occurs through coughing, aiding in the removal of these substances from the lungs.

Diaphragm - The Diaphragm is the strong wall of muscle that separates your chest cavity from your abdominal cavity. By moving downward, it creates suction to draw in air and expand the lungs.

Bronchioles - Bronchioles, the tiniest divisions of the bronchi, lead to the alveoli, which are small air sacs found at their terminus.

Alveoli - The Alveoli are the very small air sacs that are the destination of air that you breathe in.

Capillaries - The capillaries are blood vessels situated within the walls of the alveoli. Blood is carried to the capillaries by the pulmonary artery and then removed by the pulmonary vein. Within the capillaries, carbon dioxide is transferred into the alveoli, while oxygen is absorbed from the air within the alveoli.

How does the Respiratory System clean the air that we breath?

Your respiratory system possesses inherent mechanisms to prevent harmful substances in the air from reaching your lungs. The nose hairs aid in filtering out large particles, while small hair-like structures called cilia along the air passages continuously sweep to maintain cleanliness. However, exposure to harmful substances such as cigarette smoke can impair the function of cilia, potentially resulting in health issues such as bronchitis. Cells lining your trachea and bronchial tubes produce mucus, which moisturizes air passages and serves to prevent the entry of dust, bacteria, viruses, and allergens into your lungs. Mucus can bring up things that reach deeper into your lungs. You then cough out or swallow them.
